Elle Woods & The Immediate Duality

On moving and feeling like a 100-year-old baby everytime.
Published July 10, 2026 •
Writing Life Film & TV
This summer, I've spent a lot of time doing small things with the expectation that each of them, in their own little way, will pay off. One of these tasks is to catch up on shows and movies that school has forced me to neglect. While the only shows that were actually on the list was The Pitt and Abbott Elementary, it was close to impossible to ignore the massive pink advertisements for the new Legally Blonde prequel. Couple that with the close and personal connection Lexi Minitree and I have (we're from the same county), 'Elle' jumped to the top of my to watch list. I'm not done with it, but so far I am #lovingit.

There's many great things about Elle (the show, and the character). There are almost just as many things that aren't great. And while, in the grand scheme of shows, it falls into a category of fun teenage binges (albeit not the same way Off-Campus or TSITP does, more like how Derry Girls does), for me Elle brought back a lot of memories, emotions and beliefs.

To provide more context, Elle follows 16-year-old Elle Woods as the Woods family moves out of their fabulous LA life into the dreary Seattle suburbs, where everyone wears gray plad, the sun is only out for four seconds a day, and nobody likes to be a conformist (which, I'd argue, is about the same as being a conformist to a specific set of conforming rules). Needless to say, Elle in her pink and pep, sticks out like a painted sore thumb. But she's determined to make the most of it. In the span of five episodes, she has her first fake boyfriend, her first kiss, her first popular girl enemy, her first bullying experience and her first successful homecoming committee chair season. All of this before December, mind you.

Now, while Elle and I are quite different people (in style, capacity to function in a courtroom, experience with love, to name a few), I find myself connecting in one extremely important way: like Elle's jump to Seattle, a total turnaround from LA, I found myself in tall yellow buildings in South India one May, and in sunny Georgian suburbs not two months later.

With job relocations, opportunity seekers and family ties, most people will move at least once in their lives. However, there are unspoken timelines for these moves. Perhaps a student starting college out of state, or newlyweds seeking a place to start a family. Elle, at sixteen, is comfortable where she is, and does not expect the move. I, also at sixteen, had a little more of an inkling that the move was coming. But it still felt like someone was pulling at all of the roots I'd so carefully sown into my garden.

I've learn in the last twenty years that starting over is a dissociating experience. I often found myself giving my parent's Indian phone numbers out only to remember halfway through that if those numbers are still in use, they won't even go through from North Georgia. As texts from friends dwindled, I spent more and more time looking through their Instagram profiles to figure out if the last conversation we had about her plans to adopt a dog actually happened. I'd tell people in the new and strange place about the life I'd lived before, but to anyone but me it was just a story, not a real lived experience.

Elle’s live is divided into a ‘here’, the damp streets of Seattle that she slowly grows to love, and ‘there’, LA liveliness that she’s starting to feel distant from. Her friends are divided into ‘here’ friends and ‘there’ friends. She tries her hardest to get involved in a school where people try even harder to not get involved. In a way, she keeps trying to bring LA to Seattle.

In a new place, there’s an inherent desire to merge the old. This lingers forever, I’d like to claim. But in the first days, months, years, it’s blatant and unconscious. Elle wears her LA chic in a sea of gray. She calls Madison and asks behind the high school gossip. Eva (Elle’s mom), redecorates their house and throws a housewarming party no one has ever been to before. I hold on to the same music, so much so that when I’m listening to it, I’m not thinking about artists, but am almost always thinking about an old friend.

There’s probably hundreds of shows out there about the new girl at school and how she finds her place. Most of them might be better than Elle. But I started Elle, not any of the others. And I titled this piece ‘Immediate Duality’, because as it approaches four years since I’ve moved, I haven’t stopped thinking about how my life has felt like multiple strands tied together. There is the version of me that had never moved out of Seattle— who maybe goes to the University of Washington, probably a Computer Science undergraduate. There is the version of me who stayed in Bangalore, maybe in a performing arts school now. Then, one of the more prominent strands, the version that never left Hyderabad. She probably is well on her way to completing her BMBS, set on becoming a doctor. The version that did happen is somehow impossibly small, covered over by other strands of ‘what could have been’. Labelled ‘there’, ‘there’, and ‘there’. Really, in the chaos of the there’s there isn’t much space for here now. It’s more of a quad-ality, I guess? Or a mult-ality.

It’ll always be a funny thing to me, how we have the ability to pick up, restart somewhere far away, and anticipate that things will work out. I vividly remember a lot of my first days in a new place being spent walking around, and then waiting for someone to ask me to play, or giving up and reaching out to a girl I thought shared all my classes. Having that sort of blind faith gives me a sense of doom, but more a sense of hope. Moving means a new place, a loss of comfort, of familiar society, of ‘home’. But it also means the chance to explore, flourish and revel in the new air, travelling on new roads and listening to new lectures. And somehow, even though I’ve done it more times than I could count on one hand, it never gets old. That’s the true thrill of it.
